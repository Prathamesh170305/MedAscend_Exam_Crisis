CHAPTER: 1 — Legal Procedure

QUESTION:
A 56-year-old female victim of domestic violence is admitted to the hospital with severe injuries. What is the procedure undertaken as per the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (relevant section)? *(Dying Declaration / Medical Evidence)*

📝 **MODEL ANSWER (complete, exam-ready):**

**Identification of the procedure:**
The procedure that applies here is the recording of a **dying declaration** — a statement, oral or written, made by a person under the apprehension of death, regarding the cause and circumstances of her death. It is admissible as an exception to the hearsay rule under **section 26 of the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023** (corresponding to the earlier section 32 of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872).

**Legal basis & rationale:**
Under BSA §26, a statement made by a person who has subsequently died, relating to the cause of his / her death or the circumstances of the transaction that resulted in death, is a relevant fact and is admissible in any proceeding in which the cause of death comes into question. Oath is not administered because of the legal maxim **"nemo moriturus praesumitur mentiri"** — no one about to die is presumed to lie.

**Step-by-step procedure to be undertaken:**

1. **Assess the patient's medical fitness:** The treating doctor must first certify that the patient is **conscious, oriented and mentally fit** to make a statement. This certification precedes the statement and is added to the record.

2. **Inform the appropriate magistrate:** The most authoritative recorder is an **Executive or Judicial Magistrate**. The hospital authorities / police inform the duty magistrate to attend the bedside without delay.

3. **Setting at the bedside:**
   - Privacy is ensured — only the magistrate, the patient, and necessary medical staff should be present.
   - Relatives should ideally be excluded to avoid influence.
   - The patient must not be coached or led.

4. **Recording the statement:**
   - Recorded in the patient's **own words**, in question-and-answer format or as a free narrative.
   - Preferably in the language the patient speaks.
   - The cause of the injury and the identity of the perpetrator (if known) are specifically elicited.
   - **No oath** is administered.

5. **Authentication:**
   - The statement is read back to the patient.
   - Patient signs (or affixes thumb impression if too weak to sign).
   - The magistrate signs, and the doctor counter-signs, certifying that the patient remained conscious and oriented throughout.

6. **If a magistrate is not available immediately** (life is in immediate danger), the doctor or a police officer of the rank of sub-inspector or above may record the statement. The doctor's record is admissible but carries less evidentiary weight than a magistrate's record.

7. **Documentation:**
   - Original statement is forwarded to the court hearing the case.
   - A copy is kept with the medical record.
   - The doctor's fitness certificate is preserved with the statement.

**Admissibility caveats:**
- The statement becomes admissible **only if the declarant dies afterwards**. If she survives, it is treated as an ordinary statement (which can still be used in cross-examination of her later testimony).
- The patient must have been in a fit mental state at the time of recording; the fitness certificate is therefore the lynchpin.
- If the declarant was inconsistent, made multiple contradictory statements, or was under influence, the court considers all such factors before placing weight on the statement.

**Distinction from a dying deposition** (often tested alongside): If circumstances permit a more formal recording — i.e., the magistrate arranges for the accused or his pleader to be present and conducts cross-examination — the document is called a **dying deposition**, and it has higher evidentiary weight than a dying declaration. A dying deposition is taken on oath; a dying declaration is not.

⏱️ Time allocation: ~12–15 minutes

CHAPTER: 1 — Legal Procedure

QUESTION:
Describe the procedure of recording of evidence in the Court of Law.

📝 **MODEL ANSWER (complete, exam-ready):**

A witness summoned to a court of law in India undergoes a fixed five-stage procedure for recording evidence. The sequence and rules of each stage are governed by procedural law and the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023.

**Step 1 — Identification and oath:**
- The witness's name, age, occupation, and address are recorded.
- The court administers an **oath** to the witness — "I shall speak the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth." Refusal to take oath without sufficient cause is itself an offence.
- A doctor is treated as an **expert (skilled) witness** under §39 BSA 2023 (old §45 IEA).

**Step 2 — Examination-in-chief:**
- Conducted by the **party that called** the witness.
- Purpose: to elicit facts that support the calling party's case.
- The witness is asked open questions about what he saw, examined, or observed.
- **Leading questions are NOT allowed** in examination-in-chief, except with the court's permission.
- The witness should: stick to his report, answer plainly, refer to records only with the court's permission.

**Step 3 — Cross-examination:**
- Conducted by the **opposing party**.
- Purpose: to **test the credibility** of the witness, expose inconsistencies, elicit any favourable facts.
- **Leading questions ARE allowed.**
- Scope is wide — may extend beyond facts mentioned in chief, including credit-testing.
- The witness must remain composed, must not lose temper or argue, and must answer only what is asked.
- This is regarded as the most critical phase — the credibility of the witness stands or falls here.

**Step 4 — Re-examination:**
- Conducted again by the **calling party**, after cross-examination.
- Purpose: to **clarify any matter** raised in cross-examination and to rehabilitate the witness if necessary.
- **Leading questions are NOT allowed.**
- Scope is narrow — confined to matters arising in cross. **New matter** can be introduced only with **court's permission**.

**Step 5 — Court questions:**
- The presiding judge may put questions to the witness directly **at any stage** of the deposition.
- Such questions are intended to clarify points and are **not subject to objection** by either party.

**Special rule — Hostile witness:**
If during examination-in-chief the witness gives evidence against the case of the party that called him, the calling party may apply to the court to declare him a **hostile witness**. With the court's permission, the calling party may then cross-examine its own witness as if he were the opposing party's witness.

**Conduct of the doctor while deposing:**
- Be punctual; dress formally; address the court as "My Lord", "Your Honour", or "Sir" depending on the court.
- Carry the MLR, postmortem report, and case notes; refer only with permission.
- Speak clearly and slowly; answer only what is asked.
- Do not lose temper; do not volunteer information; do not speculate.
- Do not refuse to answer a court-directed question — refusal = contempt of court.
- Do not give false evidence on oath — **perjury** (punishable under §229 BNS / old §193 IPC, up to 7 years + fine).

**Mnemonic for the sequence: "OECRC"** — Oath → Examination-in-chief → Cross → Re-exam → Court questions.

⏱️ Time allocation: ~12–15 minutes

CHAPTER: 1 — Legal Procedure

QUESTION:
(a) Classify the courts in India.
(b) Differentiate between civil and criminal courts.
(c) Mention the powers of various criminal courts to impose sentence.

📝 **MODEL ANSWER (complete, exam-ready):**

**(a) Classification of courts in India:**

Courts in India are broadly classified into two streams that converge at the Supreme Court — civil and criminal — both branching downward from the Supreme Court through the High Courts to district-level courts.

**Hierarchy of Criminal Courts** (top → bottom):
1. **Supreme Court of India** — apex court of appeal; original jurisdiction in centre–state disputes.
2. **High Court** — highest court of a state; confirms death sentences passed by Sessions Courts.
3. **Sessions Court / Additional Sessions Court** — highest criminal court of a district; may pass any sentence including death (to be confirmed by HC).
4. **Assistant Sessions Court** — any sentence except death, life imprisonment, or imprisonment exceeding 10 years.
5. **Chief Judicial / Chief Metropolitan Magistrate** — any sentence except death, life imprisonment, or imprisonment exceeding 7 years.
6. **Judicial / Metropolitan Magistrate — First Class** — up to 3 years + ₹10,000 fine.
7. **Judicial Magistrate — Second Class** — up to 1 year + ₹5,000 fine.

**Hierarchy of Civil Courts** (top → bottom):
1. Supreme Court → 2. High Court (civil side) → 3. District Court (Civil Judge) → subordinate civil judges.

**(b) Civil vs Criminal courts — key differences:**

| Feature | Civil Court | Criminal Court |
|---------|--------------|------------------|
| Nature of dispute | Private (property, contract, family) | Offences against the state |
| Initiated by | Plaint by the aggrieved party | FIR / complaint; state prosecutes |
| Parties | Plaintiff vs Defendant | State vs Accused |
| Burden of proof | Preponderance of probability | **Beyond reasonable doubt** |
| Highest district court | District Court | Sessions Court |
| Outcome | Decree / compensation | Imprisonment / fine / death |
| Conduct money to witness | Paid in advance | Reimbursed by govt afterwards |

**(c) Sentencing powers of various criminal courts:**

| Court | Maximum sentence |
|-------|------------------|
| Sessions Court / Additional Sessions | **Any sentence** including death (death subject to HC confirmation) |
| Assistant Sessions Court | Up to 10 years; no death / life |
| Chief Judicial / Chief Metropolitan Magistrate | Up to 7 years + fine; no death / life |
| Judicial / Metropolitan Magistrate — 1st Class | Up to 3 years + ₹10,000 fine |
| Judicial Magistrate — 2nd Class | Up to 1 year + ₹5,000 fine |

**Note:** A death sentence is **never executable until confirmed by the High Court**, regardless of which court passed it. The Supreme Court may further hear an appeal.

QUESTION:
Define inquest. Enumerate the types of inquest. Mention which types are practised in India and the legal sections under which they are held.

📝 **MODEL ANSWER (complete, exam-ready):**

**Definition:**
An inquest is a legal inquiry or investigation conducted to ascertain the **cause and circumstances of a death** that is sudden, suspicious, or unnatural.

**Types of inquest (4):**

| Type | Conducted by | Legal section | Status in India |
|------|----------------|------------------|--------------------|
| 1. **Police inquest** | Investigating Officer (≥ Sub-Inspector) | **§174 CrPC** → §194 BNSS 2023 | ✅ Practised — most common |
| 2. **Magistrate inquest** | Executive Magistrate | **§176 CrPC** → §196 BNSS 2023 | ✅ Practised |
| 3. **Coroner inquest** | Coroner (rank of First Class Magistrate; doctor or lawyer) | Coroner's Act | ❌ Abolished — Mumbai 29 July 1999 |
| 4. **Medical Examiner system** | Medical Examiner = Forensic Pathologist | Local US statutes | ❌ Not in India (USA) |

**Police inquest — practical details:**
- The IO receives information of a sudden / suspicious / unnatural death and proceeds to the scene.
- He records his observations in the presence of **two or more responsible persons** of the locality, called **Panchas**.
- The report he prepares is the **Panchnama** — signed by the IO and the Panchas.
- He then sends the body to the government doctor for postmortem examination.

**Magistrate inquest — mandatory in:**
- Death in police custody
- Dowry death (within 7 years of marriage)
- Exhumation cases
- Death in psychiatric hospitals
- Any other sensitive case the Magistrate sees fit to inquire into

Magistrate inquest carries **higher evidentiary value** than police inquest due to the judicial standing of the officer.

**Coroner inquest (historical):**
- The Coroner could be a doctor or a lawyer.
- He examined witnesses on oath, recorded their evidence, and gave a **verdict** as to the cause of death.
- If the accused was unknown, he returned an "**open verdict**" — commission of crime confirmed but accused unidentified.
- Coroner's court was abolished in Kolkata earlier, and finally in Mumbai on 29 July 1999.

**Medical Examiner system:**
- Inquest conducted by a forensic pathologist (the "Medical Examiner").
- Considered the most scientific system; prevalent in most US states.
- Not adopted in India.

**Mnemonic — "PoMaCoMe"** in descending order of relevance to India: **Po**lice → **Ma**gistrate (both practised in India); **Co**roner → **Me**dical Examiner (not in India).

CHAPTER: 1 — Legal Procedure

QUESTION:
Define summons. Describe its types, essential contents, and the concept of conduct money.

📝 **MODEL ANSWER (complete, exam-ready):**

**Definition:**
A summons is a **written order issued by a court** directing a person to appear before it on a specified date and time, for the purpose of giving evidence or producing a document. The foreign-jurisdiction equivalent is called a **subpoena** ("under penalty").

**Essential contents of a summons:**
1. Name and designation of the issuing court.
2. Name and address of the person summoned.
3. Date, time, and place of attendance.
4. Purpose — whether to give evidence or to produce documents.
5. Signature of the presiding judge.
6. Seal of the court.
7. Issued in **duplicate** — original served on the recipient; duplicate signed and returned to the court.

**Types of summons:**

| Type | Purpose | Foreign term |
|------|---------|----------------|
| **Witness summons** | Compels the recipient to attend court and give oral evidence | "Subpoena ad testificandum" |
| **Production summons** | Compels the recipient to produce specified documents or material objects (e.g., MLR, PM report, X-rays) | "Subpoena duces tecum" |

**Conduct money:**

| Setting | Conduct money rule |
|---------|----------------------|
| **Civil cases** | Paid **in advance** to the witness — to cover travelling and subsistence expenses |
| **Criminal cases** | **No advance** payment; expenses are reimbursed by the government after the witness has attended court |

**Disobedience:**
Wilful disobedience of a duly served summons without sufficient cause amounts to **contempt of court** and is punishable. The signature of the judge and the seal of the court are essential for the summons to be legally enforceable.

CHAPTER: 1 — Legal Procedure

QUESTION:
Classify medical evidence. Define each type with examples. Mention the admissibility of hearsay evidence with exceptions.

📝 **MODEL ANSWER (complete, exam-ready):**

**Classification:**
Medical evidence is the evidence given by a medical practitioner in his professional capacity. It is classified into **five types**:

1. **Documentary evidence** — evidence in the form of documents produced before the court.
   - Examples: medical certificates (birth, death, age, fitness); medicolegal reports (MLR); postmortem reports; **dying declarations**; affidavits; X-ray films; lab reports.
   - **Admissible** as evidence.

2. **Oral evidence** — statements made by a witness in court under oath.
   - Examples: a doctor deposing about the injuries he examined; signs and gestures of a mute witness; a written statement of a witness who cannot speak.
   - **Admissible — generally considered the "best evidence"** because the witness is subject to cross-examination.

3. **Real / material evidence** — physical objects produced before the court.
   - Examples: weapons, clothing, bullets, viscera, blood-stained articles, hair samples.
   - **Admissible.**

4. **Hearsay evidence** — what a witness has heard from another person about the matter in dispute.
   - **Generally NOT admissible**, with specific exceptions (see below).

5. **Circumstantial evidence** — indirect evidence from which a fact in issue is inferred.
   - Examples: estimating time since death from rigor mortis; inferring weapon type from wound morphology; inferring poisoning from autopsy stains.
   - **Admissible.**

**Hearsay — admitted exceptions (4 main):**

| Exception | Legal basis (2023) | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------------------|-----------|
| **Dying declaration** | **BSA §26** (old IEA §32) | Person about to die is presumed to speak the truth |
| **Res gestae** | BSA equivalent of IEA §6 | Statements forming part of the same transaction, made spontaneously |
| **Entries in public records** | BSA equivalent | Routine official records — birth, death, marriage registers |
| **Expert opinion in published treatises** | BSA equivalent of IEA §45 | Standard textbooks can be referred to in court |

QUESTION:
Differentiate between (a) cognizable and non-cognizable offences and (b) bailable and non-bailable offences, with examples.

📝 **MODEL ANSWER (complete, exam-ready):**

**(a) Cognizable vs Non-cognizable offences:**

| Feature | Cognizable | Non-cognizable |
|---------|---------------|------------------|
| Police arrest | **Without warrant** | **Warrant required** |
| Investigation | Without magistrate's order | Needs magistrate's order |
| Severity | Generally serious (≥ 3 years) | Generally less serious |
| FIR | Mandatory | Optional (Non-Cognizable Report) |
| Bail | Often non-bailable | Usually bailable |
| Examples | Murder, rape, dacoity, dowry death, grievous hurt | Defamation, simple hurt, public nuisance, criminal trespass |

**(b) Bailable vs Non-bailable offences:**

| Feature | Bailable | Non-bailable |
|---------|------------|----------------|
| Bail | **A matter of right** for the accused | **Discretionary** — only the court may grant |
| Bail-granting authority | Police OR court | Only court |
| Severity | Less serious | Serious; usually cognizable too |
| Trial court | Magistrate | Sessions Court (usually) |
| Examples | Simple hurt, defamation, criminal trespass, public nuisance | Murder (BNS §103), rape (BNS §63), dacoity, dowry death |

**Combined classification (typical):**
- Cognizable + non-bailable = most serious (e.g., murder)
- Non-cognizable + bailable = least serious (e.g., defamation)

The current classification of each offence as cognizable / non-cognizable and bailable / non-bailable is laid down in the **First Schedule of BNSS 2023** (which replaces the corresponding schedule of CrPC).

CHAPTER: 1 — Legal Procedure

QUESTION:
Describe the conduct expected of a doctor while giving evidence in a court of law. Mention the legal consequences of giving false evidence.

📝 **MODEL ANSWER (complete, exam-ready):**

A doctor is summoned to court as an **expert (skilled) witness** under §39 BSA, 2023 (old §45 IEA). His credibility = the credibility of his evidence. The professional conduct expected of him on the witness stand is divided into **Do's** and **Don'ts**.

**Do's:**
1. **Be punctual** — arrive before the scheduled time.
2. **Dress formally** — convey professionalism.
3. **Address the court properly** — "My Lord" or "Your Honour" in a High Court / Supreme Court; "Sir" in subordinate courts.
4. **Carry** all relevant records — MLR, postmortem report, case notes; refer only with the court's permission.
5. **Speak clearly and slowly**, audible to the court and the recording clerk.
6. **Answer only what is asked**; confine answers to your professional knowledge.
7. **Use plain language**; explain medical terms when used.
8. **Take oath** before deposing — refusal without cause is an offence.

**Don'ts:**
1. **Do not lose temper** under cross-examination, however provocative.
2. **Do not argue** with the lawyer or the judge.
3. **Do not volunteer** additional information not asked for.
4. **Do not speculate** beyond the evidence on record.
5. **Do not disclose patient secrets** without the patient's consent OR an explicit court direction — bound by professional secrecy.
6. **Do not take sides** — remain neutral; the doctor is an expert, not an advocate.
7. **Do not refuse** a court-directed question — refusal amounts to **contempt of court**.
8. **Do not give false evidence** on oath — this is **perjury**.

**Legal consequences of false evidence (perjury):**

- Perjury = wilfully giving false evidence in any stage of a judicial proceeding while bound by oath to state the truth.
- Punishable under **§229 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023** (corresponding to the earlier §193 IPC).
- Punishment: imprisonment of either description for a term that may extend to **seven years** + fine.
- If false evidence is given in a non-judicial proceeding, imprisonment may extend to 3 years + fine.
- Additionally, the doctor faces professional consequences — **infamous conduct** under medical-council regulations, possibly leading to suspension or removal from the medical register.

**Mnemonic for Do's — "PALACE"** (treat the witness box like a palace): **P**unctual, **A**ttire, **L**anguage (address), **A**nswer only what's asked, **C**arry records, **E**xplain plainly.
**For Don'ts — "NEVER"**: **N**o losing temper, **E**vade nothing (refusal = contempt), **V**olunteer (don't), **E**xceed your evidence, **R**eveal secrets (don't).

CHAPTER: 1 — Legal Procedure

QUESTION:
Classify witnesses appearing in a court of law. Differentiate between a common witness, expert witness, and hostile witness.

📝 **MODEL ANSWER (complete, exam-ready):**

**Definition of a witness:**
A witness is any person who appears in court and gives evidence under oath about facts within his knowledge.

**Classification — three types:**

1. **Common witness (witness of fact):**
   - Deposes about facts **seen, heard, or otherwise perceived** by him.
   - **Cannot give opinion** — purely an observer.
   - Example: a passer-by who witnessed a road traffic accident; a neighbour who heard a victim's cry.

2. **Expert (skilled) witness:**
   - A person **specially skilled** in a particular branch of knowledge — science, art, foreign law, handwriting, finger impressions.
   - **Permitted to give opinion** before the court.
   - Legal basis: **§39 of the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023** (corresponding to the earlier §45 of the Indian Evidence Act).
   - Examples: a forensic doctor deposing on cause of death; a ballistics expert; a handwriting analyst; a psychiatrist.
   - **A doctor appearing in court in his professional capacity is treated as an expert witness.**

3. **Hostile witness:**
   - A witness who, after being called, **gives evidence against the case of the party that summoned him**.
   - The court must **formally declare** him hostile before cross-examination by his own side is permitted.
   - With the court's permission, the calling party may then cross-examine its own witness as if he were the opposing party's witness.
   - Often arises in cases involving threat, inducement, or compromise of the witness.

**Differentiating table:**

| Feature | Common witness | Expert witness | Hostile witness |
|---------|------------------|------------------|--------------------|
| Synonym | Witness of fact | Skilled witness | — |
| Can give opinion? | ❌ No | ✅ Yes (within expertise) | Depends |
| Legal basis (for opinion) | — | **BSA §39** (old IEA §45) | — |
| Oath required | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes |
| Subject to cross-examination | ✅ Yes (by opposing side) | ✅ Yes (by opposing side) | ✅ Yes — **and by his own side** with court's permission |
| Role of judge | Receives factual testimony | Receives opinion evidence; weighs as appropriate | **Must formally declare** as "hostile" first |
| Example | Eyewitness to a fall | Forensic doctor on cause of death | Complainant who, mid-trial, denies police version |

**Medicolegal relevance:**
- A doctor giving evidence in court is **always an expert witness**.
- He should be prepared to give his opinion on injuries, cause of death, fitness, age estimation, sexual-assault findings, intoxication, etc., based on his examination and the records.
- His opinion is given weight by the court but is not binding — the court ultimately decides on the totality of evidence.
