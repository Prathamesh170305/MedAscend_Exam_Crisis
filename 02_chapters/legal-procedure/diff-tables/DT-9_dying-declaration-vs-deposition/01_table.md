**DT-9 — Dying Declaration vs Dying Deposition**
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

> The single most-tested differentiation in Legal Procedure. Mastering this table = mastering an exam favourite.

| Feature | Dying Declaration | Dying Deposition |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Definition** | Statement of a dying person about the **cause / circumstances of his death** | Statement of a dying person recorded **in the form of a court deposition** |
| **Recorded by** | Magistrate (ideal) → otherwise doctor / police officer | **Magistrate** only |
| **Oath** | ❌ **NOT required** — "nemo moriturus praesumitur mentiri" | ✅ **Required** |
| **Accused / pleader present?** | ❌ NOT present | ✅ **Accused or his pleader is present** |
| **Cross-examination?** | ❌ NOT done | ✅ **Done** |
| **Format** | Witness's own words, signed (or thumb-impression) by declarant | Standard court deposition format — Q & A |
| **Validity** | Admissible **only if the declarant dies** afterwards | Admissible regardless — even if declarant survives later |
| **Evidentiary weight** | Strong but with caveats — corroboration sometimes sought | **Higher** — equivalent to court deposition |
| **Practical setting** | Bedside, ward, scene of crime | Bedside, but with formal court arrangements |
| **Current legal basis** | **BSA §26 (2023)** *(old IEA §32)* — exception to hearsay | Procedural — under BNSS / BSA provisions |
| **When chosen** | When patient is critically ill; little time | When patient is too ill to attend court but lives long enough for full deposition |
| **Common pitfall** | Recorded by police without magistrate → still admissible but weaker | — |
