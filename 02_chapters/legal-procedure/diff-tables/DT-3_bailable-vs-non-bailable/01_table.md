**DT-3 — Bailable vs Non-bailable Offence**
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

| Feature | Bailable Offence | Non-bailable Offence |
|---------|--------------------|------------------------|
| **Definition** | Offence in which **bail is a matter of right** for the accused | Offence in which bail is **not a right** — discretion of the court |
| **Bail-granting authority** | Police officer or court — must grant on application | Only court can grant; usually denied for serious offences |
| **Examples** | Simple hurt, defamation, public nuisance, criminal trespass | Murder (BNS §103), rape (BNS §63), dacoity, dowry death |
| **Severity** | Less serious | Serious — generally cognizable too |
| **Bond / surety** | Personal bond / surety furnished | If granted, conditions are stringent; large bonds or sureties |
| **Trial court** | Magistrate Court | Sessions Court (usually) |
| **Police power on arrest** | Must release on bail once bond is furnished | Police cannot grant bail — must produce before magistrate |
| **Status under new code** | BNSS 2023 — Schedule classifies each offence as bailable / non-bailable | BNSS 2023 — Schedule classifies each offence |
| **Overlap with cognizable** | Some bailable offences are cognizable (e.g., riot) | Most non-bailable offences are also cognizable |
